Future Flood Prevention and Fund Distribution in Toronto

Author: Livia Hu, Report date: August 15th, 2024

The intensification of urban flooding has emerged as a significant challenge for Toronto and various other municipalities across Ontario (Global News, 2024; CBC News, 2024; Sills et al., 2013).¹ As climate change continues to impact the region, cities throughout the province are experiencing a steady increase in rainfall intensity, which often leads to excessive flooding. Within Toronto, established mitigation strategies—including the Wet Weather Flow Master Plan (WWFMP) and financial support initiatives such as the recurring “No Fault Grant” program—have played a crucial role in reducing flood-related damage.

This report utilizes data from ClimateData.ca to evaluate the strategic allocation of flood relief funds in the coming years. It specifically analyzes the highest 5-day precipitation trends (Rx5day) among major Ontario cities to guide distribution. To achieve higher precision for local planning, a secondary dataset focused on July rainfall in Toronto was analyzed to pinpoint specific peak intensity years. As data analysts for Ontario Government Fund Disbursement Organizations, our objective is to optimize the “No Fault Grant” funding allocation by considering a longer timeframe and the needs of adjacent cities. Our research examined a comprehensive range using “30-year average annual data” from 1951 to 2071², focusing on the five largest Ontario cities: Hamilton, Mississauga, Brampton, Toronto, and Ottawa. To ensure accurate projections, the study focused on a low-emission (green pathway) scenario at the 90th rainfall percentile, represented in the dataset as ssp126_rx5day_p90².

Precipitation Trend Analysis and Allocation

Climate projections indicate varying precipitation trends across the province, necessitating a dynamic and responsive funding strategy. Data for four of the studied cities—Hamilton, Mississauga, Brampton, and Toronto—shows a synchronized pattern where 5-day maximum precipitation is expected to peak between the 2031–2060 period before gradually declining toward the end of the century. In contrast, Ottawa’s forecast remains relatively stable through 2060 but shows a sharp, significant increase between the 2041–2070 and 2071–2100 periods.

(Figure 1: 5-Day Maximum Precipitation (ssp126, p90) of Major Cities in Ontario)

These variations highlight the need for adaptable funding models. Cities in the GTA cluster (Hamilton, Mississauga, Brampton, and Toronto) should receive expanded funding budgets in the immediate future until they reach their projected rainfall peaks. Conversely, Ottawa may maintain its current funding levels until future reports indicate the start of its projected significant upward trend. While inter‑city comparisons suggest that Toronto does not have a single clear priority for fund allocation based on these rankings, the significant rise in its 5‑day maximum precipitation in recent years supports the need for a continued and expanded flood‑management budget for the city³.

(Figure 2: 5-Day Maximum Precipitation (ssp126, p90) in Toronto)

Toronto-Specific Peak and Mitigation Strategies

To identify specific high-risk windows for Toronto, our team analyzed July data, as it is historically the wettest month in Ontario. Projections from this dataset suggest that the city will experience a significant 5-day precipitation peak in 2035⁴.

(Figure 3: Toronto Yearly Precipitation (ssp126, p90) Present-2060)        

While extreme precipitation is a primary driver of flooding, its impact should not be overstated in isolation. Effective disaster management must also account for vegetation, topography, existing drainage infrastructure, and the speed of emergency rescue responses. In instances of budget constraints, the city can implement lower-cost, proactive measures to remediate disaster risks:

  • Stormwater Management: Guiding municipal operations, environmental monitoring, and public education efforts.
  • Educational Integration: Implementing tree planting and environmental maintenance activities in primary and secondary school curricula.
  • Infrastructure Maintenance: Prioritizing the detection and self-repair of cracks in foundations, basement walls, or basement windows and doors.
  • Green Infrastructure: Encouraging the adoption of “green roofs” to manage stormwater sustainably.

In conclusion, this report advocates for a dynamic flood funding model based on projected climatological trends. While most Ontario cities, including Toronto, require increased budgets to combat rising precipitation, Toronto must prepare for its specific mid-century peak. By advancing civic awareness and enhancing disaster response effectiveness, the city can better navigate potential budget shortfalls while building long-term resilience.
